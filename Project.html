Project - Understanding the decline in global fertility rates

Aims of Projects

Fertility rates are very important for the stability and growth of an economy. As fertility rates fall, working-age populations eventually fall as well, which can lead to labor shortages in countries where the working-age populations are in decline. This is why some see the current situation of declining fertility rates as a real issue. The main aims of this project are to evaluate the accuracy of some of the most commonly cited reasons behind the convergence in fertility rates and to show why there is infact differences in fertility rates amongst countries.

Figure 1 is a line chart showing the fertility rate changes experienced by countries across the world with the data coming from the World Bank. Quality of Healthcare

Figure 2 - Child Mortality Rates

The first commonly cited reason as to why fertility rates have decreased, is due to declining child mortality rates. The idea is that when child mortality rates are high, the fertility rate remains high since parents are not sure about the survival of their children. This behaviour is shown in the chart as there seems to be a strong correlation between declining child mortality rates and declining fertility rates.

Figure 3 - Access to contraception

Another commonly cited reason as to why fertility rates have decreased, is due to poor access to contraception. The world map shows that poor access to contraception generally appears in Africa, which as shown in the line chart is the continent with the highest fertiltiy rates; so there does seem to be correlation between the two.

Changing Gender Roles

Figure 4 - Increasing Female Education

This chart looks at the relationship between the years in school a women is expected to face and fertility rates, with each data point having its size related to the nation's population. The theory is that with greater education, women would have greater labour-market opportunities which provides them with a route other than motherhood. Based solely on this graph, there is a strong correlation between the two factors. However, the chart showing female labour force participation and fertilty rates doesn't show provide evidence for the theory; while the majority of countries do see their fertility rates decline, this isn't always in the face of increasing labour force participation, with most african countries seeing labour force participation decline.
